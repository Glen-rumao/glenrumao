A Lesson, Not a Footnote

Building Beyond Code

Tyro
Startup Attempt

A while back, I co-founded and worked on a startup called Tyro — an ambitious attempt to build something meaningful from the ground up. We had the energy, the technical ability, and a genuine belief in the problem we were solving. Tyro was a startup that was built to help freshers and early-career professionals find internships and jobs. It was build on problem statements of some existing platforms and was also designed for the benifits of the companies.

Tyro ultimately did not succeed. But looking back, I can say with confidence:

"Tyro taught me more than many successful projects could have."

Building Tyro put me face-to-face with realities that no course, tutorial, or side project ever could. It wasn't a failure of effort — it was a crash course in everything that sits between "we built something" and "people actually want and use this."

Product Validation Building something is easy. Validating that it solves a real problem, before investing months into it, is the hard part.
Customer Needs What users say they want and what they'll actually pay for or stick with are often very different things.
Market Realities A good idea on paper still has to survive contact with timing, competition, and a market that may not be ready.
Execution Challenges Shipping fast and shipping the right thing are two different skills — and both matter.
Startup Decision-Making Every decision is made with incomplete information. Learning to decide anyway, and adjust quickly, is the real skill.
Distribution A great product with no way to reach people is just a private project. Distribution isn't an afterthought — it's part of the product.
Solving Real Problems The closer you are to a real, painful problem, the more resilient your motivation — and your product — tends to be.

If anything, Tyro didn't push me away from building businesses — it pulled me closer. It's a big part of why my interest in product thinking, strategy, and operations isn't theoretical. It's earned.

Selected Work

Projects & Case Studies

A few projects that reflect how I think about problems — not just how I solve them technically.

Case 01

Internship & Skill Management System

Django DRF SQL REST APIs
Problem

Students and institutions had no structured way to track internships, the skills gained during them, and how those skills mapped to career readiness — everything lived in spreadsheets and scattered records.

Solution

Built a Django-based system with a relational data model connecting students, internships, and skill records, exposed through REST APIs so the data could be queried, filtered, and reported on consistently.

Impact

Replaced manual tracking with a single source of truth, making it possible to see skill gaps and internship activity at a glance — turning scattered data into something usable for decisions.

Key Learnings

Good database design isn't just about storing data correctly — it's about anticipating the questions people will eventually want answered.

Case 02

Invoice Generation System

Python Django Database Design
Problem

Manually creating invoices was slow, inconsistent, and error-prone — small formatting mistakes had real downstream consequences for billing and record-keeping.

Solution

Designed and built an automated invoice generation system, with a backend data model that handled clients, line items, and totals consistently, removing manual calculation and formatting from the process.

Impact

Cut the time and error rate involved in generating invoices, while creating a consistent, professional output every time — a small system with an outsized effect on day-to-day reliability.

Key Learnings

Automation isn't just a technical convenience — for businesses, it's often the difference between something feeling professional or amateur.

Case 03

Quizzyverse

Django SQL Backend Architecture
Problem

No proper system for quizzes and assessments — leading to inconsistent evaluation and a fragmented user experience.

Solution

Built a quiz and assessment system where vast categories of quizzes were available and also teachers had the ability to create and manage their own quizzes. Implemented a special leaderboard feature to encourage engagement with a unique feature of instagram id on the leaderboard.

Impact

Improved the overall user experience for both students and teachers, providing a more consistent and engaging platform for quizzes and assessments and motivating students to learn and win.

Key Learnings

Systems that touch people's evaluations carry real responsibility — the architecture has to be both technically sound and fair by design.
